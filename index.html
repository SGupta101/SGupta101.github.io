My path as a computer scientist

I had two very good things growing up. The first of these is that I had three computer scientists to look up to: my mom, my dad, and my brother. The second is that I got to be a computer scientist someone else could look up to. As a computer scientist I've explored my interest by taking classes such as Artificial Intelligence and teaching computer science to elementary school girls on free or reduced lunch. I've interned at places like digital advocacy startup Phone2Action and the GMU ASSIP research program, and have learned a lot about the value of my voice. One of my favorite places to code is at hackathons (yes, my main reason might be because of the free food).

My path as a journalist

It isn't until I joined tjMedia my sophomore year that I realized I love to write. I am currently an Editor-in-Chief for my school newspaper, for which I lead our print publication as well as write articles and design spreads. My favorite part about writing is learning about the different perspectives of the people and communities I write about.

How my two paths led me to computational journalism

As I continued on the path through both fields I soon realized I don't have to pick one; it doesn't have to be a choice between STEM or humanities.

Why computational journalism is important to me

Especially in confusing times as these, with COVID-19 and the Black Lives Matter Movement, it's important for journalists to continue reporting while also maintaining trust with their readers. The computer science aspect appeals to logos and ethos through the use of tools such as data and algorithmic verification and allows the readers to get a sense of the bigger picture. The journalism aspect appeals to pathos and dives into the details of the story.

What is computational journalism?

Computational journalism is journalism with the incorporation of algorithms to better gather and display data as well as deliver more accurate news without bias. It has the future of unraveling complex stories in a more efficient and understandable way, and encourages better journalists and less political polarization.

What I've done in computational journalism

With Tableau, I've investigated stories such as mental health funding at TJ and the trends in Gen Z compared to previous generations. For my senior research project, I am currently working on analyzing event selection across news outlets. The end-product will be a website outputting a matrix news chart, where each cell contains an article from the respective news outlet on the respective event, allowing the user to see the differences in the way user-inputted news outlets cover specific events.
